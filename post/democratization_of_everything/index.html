democratisation of everything

November 3, 2016

You’re hungry, and the year is 90,000 BC. What do you do? How do you live?

More broadly - how do you make a living? How many possible “professions” are there - ten or twenty perhaps? It’s likely you’re roaming, hunting, gathering, or possibly fishing. There’s a nearly 1-to-1 ratio of who finds the food to who consumes it.

Fast forward to 8000 BC where the “Paleo” fad is finally over. You and your fellow nomads discover how breed crops and animals, you begin to settle down. This is a critical milestone in human history; a tipping point where that “nearly 1-to-1 ratio” begins to change. Farming yields surplus food which allows for a population boom and frees up minds for more interesting problems; like irrigation, architecture, and studying the weather. As the small settlement grows into a village, then town, the diversity of talents flourish; this can be considered the beginning of specialisation.

Stay with me as you wake up in the the Song dynasty, 1000 AD, where culture is booming. You have a society; and it has a military, religions, science, government, and more. As technology adopts and education spreads, fewer people are needed to satisfy the populations’ fundamental needs. Agriculture is becoming competitive, and some are driven to discover new niches; consequently spreading new ideas, practice, products, and cuisines. With reduced cost of living, you start to indulge in luxuries once available only to the most privileged.

A trend emerges—do you see it yet? Increasing productivity and accessibility of fundamentals, paves the way for progress elsewhere.

Moving ahead to 1700 AD. You’re still working in agriculture; in fact, the majority of the western world is. Although, don’t get too attached, the Industrial Revolution is about to make a drastic impact on everything.

“every dimension of human life, was affected by the Second Industrial Revolution” - Robert Gordon in Freakonomics (I recommend subscribing to this one)

This graph is fascinating at face value — the incredible transition in the share of population required to support the whole, brought on by greater productivity of fewer but more specialised groups. What’s even more interesting, is how the same logic can be applied elsewhere. Let’s continue forward to the 21st century where a new commodity is in demand—technology:

It’s 2006 and you use “wizardry” to communicate through space, fly over oceans, and see bones through muscle — and now you need a website; everyone is doing it. You hire a local IT professional for the job and they quickly get to work.

Just like Carl Sagan said about baking an apple pie from scratch, you must first invent the universe. Well, if your’re going to build a website from scratch, first you need some hardware, and it’ll need an Operating System, and a web server, a database, a connection to the internet, and a domain name. Only then can you begin with your website.

The Democratisation of Technology

You may have heard of “Cloud Computing” - and that it’s a big deal.

It boils down to this; compare our 2005 scenario of needing a website—and the daunting pre-requisites—to the same requirement today:

If you want a website in 2016, you don’t take out a loan and seek your nearest IT professional. Instead, using services, you get the world’s best team of engineers to architect and configure highly resilient and secure infrastructure. You don’t spend of a bag of cash for depreciating hardware; you pay cents an hour for highly tuned hardware, and you get it now.

This is “Infrastructure as a Service,” and like any other service you use it as required, pay the bill, and stop using it when you’re done. If you need more of it, you pay more; and if you want a better price, you can lock yourself into a one to three-year contract.

To reference our agriculture analogy:

Now that fewer, more experienced farmers are taking care of the fundamentals, you can focus on solving more interesting problems.

Or in this case; now that somebody more experienced with building datacenters is taking care of your hardware, you can begin on your website immediately!

The Democratisation of Knowledge

Arguably, this began in the 16th century with the widespread adoption of the printing press. The printing press changed writing into something that was replicable, referencable, citable; and as such became important to also record who wrote the text and when. The printing industry boomed and industrial scale printing paved the way for “The Press;” now the common man could be better informed.

Similarly, in the late 20th century we saw another dramatic towards accessible knowledge—The Internet. It’s easy to take for granted how available and open the internet is, but it wasn’t by accident. Just Listen or watch a talk by Tim Berners-Lee, one of the “fathers of the internet,” he quite literally designed it that way.

If you’re about my age or older, you probably remember a time before “Google” was a verb and when Encyclopedias had mass. There were two primary sources of information back then; Mum and Dad, and the the school library. Following that you could ask a teacher in that respective field, but the quality of the answer was highly dependent on that individual’s capability.

Then in 2001, Wikipedia was born. By 2003 it measured 100,000 English articles, peer reviewed and publicly available for no charge. Creator, Jimmy Wales, unexpectedly stumbled onto a very powerful psychological quirk. As described by Dan Ariely in Predictably Irrational; people respond well to doing work as a favour. Across the world people are falling for this allure; to build and organise a global body of knowledge, and they do it as a favour back to the world.

The Democratisation of Expertise

You have access to infrastructure, you have the world’s knowledge at your fingertips, but you still need that website; now what?

Well, the software pioneers of the late 20th century introduced one more bag of tricks—Open-Source. This one really blows my mind—and has been explained by better elsewhere—as our society would simply not exist as it does today without Open Source projects and it’s vast catalog.

When tasked with building your website, your 2016 IT guru will stand squarely on the shoulders of thousands of IT giants, download a copy of a Linux distribution of choice, the latest copy of the world’s most popular Content Management System, load up on whichever libraries they want, and get to work on your website—all without spending a dime.

In fact, they’ve already done it; people have already democratised this expertise and exposed it as service for your consumption, just search for “blog” and you’ll see.

Conclusion

It’s 2016 and you’re hungry, what do you do?

That’s a really tough question, one which my nieces and nephews deliberate as they apply for university. There is a broader range of possible occupations than ever before, and the trend will continue, so all I can say is this:

  1. Learning agility is critical; things are changing rapidly
  2. Don’t re-invent the wheel, printing press, or web server; stand on those shoulders
  3. Give back to humanity; be the next shoulder
